## The Big Picture

Somewhere in the history of the early universe, something went wonderfully right. Matter and antimatter should have been created in equal measure at the Big Bang, annihilating each other into a featureless sea of radiation. Instead, a tiny asymmetry survived, and that leftover sliver of matter became everything: stars, planets, you. Physicists have spent decades hunting for the mechanism behind this asymmetry, and one of the most compelling suspects is a ghost particle that barely interacts with anything at all.

**Heavy neutral leptons (HNLs)** are hypothetical particles that appear in some of the simplest extensions of the Standard Model — the reigning theory of fundamental particles and forces. Unlike the three known neutrino types, HNLs would be far heavier and would couple only faintly to ordinary matter. That faint coupling is precisely what makes them interesting: in one stroke, HNLs could explain why neutrinos have mass at all, and could drive the matter-antimatter asymmetry through **leptogenesis** — a process in which particle-antiparticle imbalances in the early universe seeded today's matter-dominated cosmos. They might even contribute to dark matter.

The catch: HNLs interact so feebly that finding them requires enormous detectors and creative strategies. A new analysis from the MicroBooNE collaboration at Fermilab has set the most stringent constraints yet on one class of these particles in a key mass range, and completed the first-ever direct search for a specific decay mode no experiment had previously targeted.

> **Key Insight:** MicroBooNE has established the tightest limits on heavy neutral lepton mixing in the 35–175 MeV mass range and conducted the world's first direct search for HNLs decaying into a neutrino and a neutral pion — closing a significant gap in the global hunt for physics beyond the Standard Model.

## How It Works

The MicroBooNE detector sits at Fermilab in Illinois and belongs to a class of instruments called **liquid-argon time projection chambers (LArTPC)**. Think of it as a 170-ton camera filled with ultra-pure liquid argon, capable of tracking charged particles with millimeter precision. When a particle passes through, it ionizes the argon atoms, and the freed electrons drift toward wire planes that reconstruct a three-dimensional image of the event — like an X-ray CT scanner for subatomic collisions.

![Figure 1](/iaifi-research-blog/figures/2310_07660/figure_1.png)

For this search, the collaboration used data from the **NuMI beam** (Neutrinos at the Main Injector), a high-energy neutrino beam fired from Fermilab's Main Injector. The choice was deliberate: NuMI's higher beam energy produces more kaons, which are primary factories for HNL production. The dataset corresponds to **7.01 × 10²⁰ protons on target** — a measure of the enormous number of proton collisions used to generate the beam, accumulated between 2015 and 2021.

The analysis targets two distinct HNL decay channels within a unified framework:

- **νe⁺e⁻ channel**: An HNL decays into a neutrino plus an electron and its antimatter counterpart, a positron. This covers HNL masses from 10 to 150 MeV.
- **νπ⁰ channel**: An HNL decays into a neutrino and a neutral pion, which immediately decays into two photons (π⁰ → γγ). This covers masses from 150 to 245 MeV.

![Figure 2](/iaifi-research-blog/figures/2310_07660/figure_2.png)

Both channels produce two **electromagnetic showers** — cascades of particles and light that form when high-energy electrons or photons enter the argon. This visual similarity allowed the team to build a unified pipeline using **boosted decision trees (BDTs)**, an ensemble machine learning technique that combines many simple classifiers into a powerful discriminant. The BDTs were trained to separate genuine HNL-like events from the far more numerous background neutrino interactions, using features like shower angles, energy deposits, and decay-point geometry.

The search assumes only the muon-flavor mixing parameter **|Uμ4|²** — measuring how strongly HNLs couple to muon-neutrinos — is nonzero. HNLs would be produced through muon-neutrino interactions in the beam, travel some distance, then decay inside the detector. The analysis sets **90% confidence level upper limits** on |Uμ4|² across both mass ranges, ruling out any HNL coupling stronger than these bounds with 90% confidence. Both signal and background processes were modeled using detailed **Monte Carlo simulations** — computer models that simulate millions of particle interactions through statistical sampling.

![Figure 3](/iaifi-research-blog/figures/2310_07660/figure_3.png)

## Why It Matters

The results push the experimental frontier in two ways. In the mass range 35 to 175 MeV, MicroBooNE now holds the world's most stringent direct constraints on the muon-mixing parameter, surpassing every previous experiment. This mass window has historically been difficult to probe — it sits between the reach of older decay experiments and the sensitivity of collider searches.

Second, the νπ⁰ channel had simply never been targeted by a direct search before. This analysis establishes the first experimental constraints from that decay mode, opening a new avenue in the global HNL exclusion program.

Beyond the specific numbers, this result demonstrates the power of liquid-argon technology for new-physics searches. The same capabilities — fine-grained tracking, energy-measuring imaging, particle identification — that make LArTPCs ideal for neutrino oscillation studies also enable precision searches for rare decays. As the broader **Short-Baseline Neutrino (SBN) program** comes fully online with larger detectors like SBND and ICARUS, these techniques will scale to even greater sensitivity. Future experiments such as DUNE, with its massive far detector, could eventually close off much of the remaining range of possible HNL properties in this mass regime.

> **Bottom Line:** MicroBooNE has delivered the world's tightest constraints on heavy neutral leptons in a critical mass window and opened a new experimental frontier with the first direct search for the νπ⁰ decay mode — bringing physicists closer to answering whether these ghostly particles hold the key to the universe's matter-antimatter imbalance.
