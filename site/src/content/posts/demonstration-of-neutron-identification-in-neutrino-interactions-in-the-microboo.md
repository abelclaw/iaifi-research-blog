abstract: A significant challenge in measurements of neutrino oscillations is reconstructing
  the incoming neutrino energies. While modern fully-active tracking calorimeters
  such as liquid argon time projection chambers in principle allow the measurement
  of all final state particles above some detection threshold, undetected neutrons
  remain a considerable source of missing energy with little to no data constraining
  their production rates and kinematics. We present the first demonstration of tagging
  neutrino-induced neutrons in liquid argon time projection chambers using secondary
  protons emitted from neutron-argon interactions in the MicroBooNE detector. We describe
  the method developed to identify neutrino-induced neutrons and demonstrate its performance
  using neutrons produced in muon-neutrino charged current interactions. The method
  is validated using a small subset of MicroBooNE's total dataset. The selection yields
  a sample with $60\%$ of selected tracks corresponding to neutron-induced secondary
  protons.

## The Big Picture

Imagine trying to reconstruct a crime scene when some of the key evidence is invisible. You can see the aftermath — marks on the floor, disturbed furniture — but the perpetrator left no fingerprints and no photograph. Now imagine that perpetrator is a subatomic particle, the whole event lasts a fraction of a nanosecond, and your entire understanding of why the universe is made of matter — rather than nothing at all — depends on catching it in the act.

That's precisely the challenge physicists face with neutrons.

Neutrinos are among the most ghostly particles in the universe, but they do occasionally interact with matter and produce a shower of secondary particles. Measuring the energy of those secondary particles tells us the energy of the original neutrino — critical for experiments probing whether neutrinos and antineutrinos behave differently, which could explain why the universe is made of matter at all. The snag: neutrons, being electrically neutral, leave no track in detectors the way charged particles do. They carry away energy in silence, systematically skewing every neutrino energy measurement made.

The MicroBooNE collaboration has now achieved a landmark first: demonstrating that neutrino-induced neutrons *can* be tagged inside a liquid argon time projection chamber, by hunting for the secondary protons kicked out when those invisible neutrons slam into argon nuclei.

> **Key Insight:** For the first time, physicists have demonstrated a method to identify neutrons produced in neutrino interactions inside a liquid argon detector — the same technology powering the next generation of neutrino experiments — achieving a sample where 60% of selected particle tracks correspond to genuine neutron-induced secondary protons.

## How It Works

The **MicroBooNE detector** is a 170-tonne tank of ultra-pure liquid argon threaded with thousands of sensing wires. When a charged particle passes through, it strips electrons from argon atoms; those freed electrons drift toward the wires, creating a detailed three-dimensional image of the interaction. This technology — the **liquid argon time projection chamber (LArTPC)** — is extraordinarily powerful for tracking charged particles, but it has a blind spot: neutral particles like neutrons don't ionize argon, so they leave no track at all.

The key insight is indirect detection. A neutron speeding through liquid argon will eventually collide with an argon nucleus and knock out a proton — and *that* proton is charged, leaving a clean track in the detector. By searching for these **neutron-induced secondary protons (NISPs)**, the team can infer a neutron's presence even though the neutron itself remained invisible.

![Figure 1](/iaifi-research-blog/figures/2406_10583/figure_1.png)

The detection strategy unfolds in several steps:

1. **Identify the neutrino interaction vertex** — the point where the original muon-neutrino interacted with an argon nucleus, producing a muon and other particles.
2. **Search for displaced proton tracks** — short tracks starting *away* from the interaction vertex, the hallmark signature of a neutron that traveled some distance before scattering.
3. **Apply selection cuts** — tracks must be spatially separated from the main vertex, have length and energy deposition consistent with a slow-moving proton, and point back toward the interaction region.

The challenge is that many short proton-like tracks arise from other sources — particles called pions bouncing off nuclei, misidentified muon segments, or ordinary protons from the primary collision placed at the wrong location. Separating genuine NISPs from this background required careful study of the **neutron interaction length** in argon — roughly 40 cm, the average distance a neutron travels before hitting a nucleus — and the characteristic energy-loss pattern of stopping protons.

![Figure 3](/iaifi-research-blog/figures/2406_10583/figure_2.png)

The team validated their method using **muon-neutrino charged current interactions**, where an incoming muon-neutrino produces a muon plus other particles. These are the best-understood neutrino collisions, making them ideal for a proof-of-concept test. Simulations using **GENIE** (which models how neutrinos interact with atomic nuclei) and **GEANT4** (which tracks how resulting particles travel through detector material) predicted the neutron production and scattering rates. The data showed good agreement with these predictions.

![Figure 5](/iaifi-research-blog/figures/2406_10583/figure_3.png)

## Why It Matters

The timing of this result is not accidental. Two enormous experiments — the **Deep Underground Neutrino Experiment (DUNE)** in South Dakota and **Hyper-Kamiokande** in Japan — are under construction and represent humanity's best shot at measuring the **CP-violating phase**, the asymmetry between neutrinos and antineutrinos that could explain why the universe contains matter rather than nothing. Both experiments demand unprecedented precision in neutrino energy reconstruction. For DUNE in particular, which uses LArTPC technology identical in principle to MicroBooNE, neutrons represent a systematic error in energy estimates that has, until now, been impossible to constrain directly from data.

This demonstration opens a new door. Future analyses can use neutron tagging to measure how many neutrons different neutrino interactions produce, what energies they carry, and how those rates compare to theory. Every improvement in neutron modeling translates directly into a sharper measurement of how neutrinos change type as they travel — and a clearer window onto the fundamental matter-antimatter asymmetry that allowed our universe to exist.

> **Bottom Line:** MicroBooNE has demonstrated the first data-driven technique to identify neutrons in a liquid argon neutrino detector, achieving 60% purity in a neutron-tagged sample — a crucial step toward eliminating the "missing energy" problem that plagues neutrino oscillation measurements at DUNE and other next-generation experiments.
